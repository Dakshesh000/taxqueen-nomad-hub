**DIGITAL NOMAD & RV LIFE FAQs:**

Q: "How does changing my lifestyle affect my state residency?"
A: "A digital nomad lifestyle allows you to choose to 'move' to a tax-free state like TX or FL. If you move to another country with a legal visa or residency permit, you would have to learn that country's taxation laws but you still might want to pick a tax-free state to remove that filing and tax obligation from your US taxes before leaving the US."

Q: "What's the difference between domicile vs where I spend time? Where do I pay state taxes?"
A: "Domicile is your home state or resident state. If you're traveling this is most likely not where you're spending most of your time. You pay state taxes if you're physically working in that state, have rental property and income or any other source of income from a particular state. You usually will submit taxes based on your domicile but if you work in multiple states, you'll have to file in those states as a nonresident."

Q: "As a digital nomad, where do I pay taxes?"
A: "If you're a US citizen, you always FILE with the US. You could possibly pay to the country you're living in if you're an expat or to a state if you're working or spending too much time in one spot. This varies according to country and/or state."

Q: "When do I owe state taxes if I'm traveling through many states?"
A: "It depends on income and if you're physically working on location in that state. This is unique to each situation and each state."

Q: "Can my RV or digital-nomad setup qualify as a home office when traveling?"
A: "No. This is very rare. If you have a toyhauler or bunkhouse RV model with a 100% dedicated home office space, then it's possible."

Q: "How do we deduct our RV if we work from it and use it for business? Can we use Section 179?"
A: "You don't deduct an RV. It is too small of a space to have 100% dedicated office space. No one gets to deduct their full home for a business."

Q: "Why can't gate guards deduct the cost of their RV? We can't do the job without the RV."
A: "Because the RV is your HOME. No one can deduct the full cost of their home."

Q: "Should my business own my RV?"
A: "No. If you're living in your RV full time, it isn't a business expenseâ€”it's your home. While it might sound like a good idea, having a business own an RV can create complications like requiring commercial insurance and added DOT stops on the highway."

Q: "Can I save on taxes with a Montana LLC?"
A: "No. While the Montana LLC is a legal thing in Montana, most other states see it as tax evasion. You also would need commercial insurance and be subject to DOT inspections since you'd be a commercial vehicle."

Q: "I'm a W-2 employee â€” which state is best to avoid state taxes?"
A: "I recommend either TX or FL for a W-2 employee."

Q: "We own property in another state â€” will we still owe state taxes if we move our domicile to TX, FL, or SD?"
A: "Possibly if you are renting that property and making money on it."

Q: "How should I file if I earned income in multiple states?"
A: "If you physically work and earn money in different states, you'll probably owe income tax to those states. This means you'll file multiple state returns. If you work remotely as a W-2 employee or run your business remotely while traveling, there's usually no need to worry about filing in multiple states."

**BUSINESS STRUCTURE FAQs:**

Q: "I'm starting a new business â€” how do I set up an LLC and make sure I start off right?"
A: "An LLC is a legal entity and is always formed with the state. It's not required to run a business."

Q: "Should I be an S-Corp or an LLC?"
A: "LLC is a legal entity, not a taxable one. S Corp is an election for taxation."

Q: "Should I be an LLC or an S corp? What's the difference?"
A: "An LLC is not a taxable business entity, so as far as the IRS is concerned, it's essentially the same thing as a sole proprietorship or a DBA. Yes, you read that rightâ€”an LLC alone offers no tangible tax benefits. An S corp can provide tax savings, assuming your business meets the qualifications."

Q: "Can my business own my RV or vehicle?"
A: "I almost never recommend your business owning your RV because you will be subject to DOT stops and inspections, commercial insurance, etc. and there's no benefit when you use your RV for personal use."

Q: "If I'm an LLC, who pays the taxes â€” me or the business?"
A: "The LLC is a legal entity. The shareholder or owners of the business are responsible for the taxes. That means you are responsible for your share of the taxes."

Q: "What is the LLC filing deadline? Why is it earlier for partnerships or S-Corps?"
A: "A single member LLC is a sole proprietor for tax purposes and files a Schedule C with your regular personal return 1040. Therefore, you have the same filing deadline as the 1040, April 15. A multi-member LLC is a partnership by default and the filing deadline is March 15. The S Corp filing deadline is March 15."

Q: "Where should I register my business/LLC?"
A: "Short answer it should be the same as your domicile or resident state. There are always exceptions to this though."

Q: "As a 1099 contractor, how can I use my LLC to save money on taxes?"
A: "LLC is a legal entity. Contrary to popular belief it offers no tax benefits."

Q: "I have an LLC with a partner â€” do we both need to take a salary? What about distributions?"
A: "LLC is a legal entity. If you're a partnership, then no partner ever gets a salary. If the LLC is taxed as an S Corp, then yes. Owners of an S Corp who work in the business are required to have a salary. Distributions are just that. They should always be tracked."

Q: "Do S-Corp distributions have to be the same for all shareholders?"
A: "S Corp distributions are always based on the % of ownership. If there is 50/50, then the distribution would be even. If it's 90/10, then the distribution would be split 90/10."

Q: "We're a married couple with an LLC partnership. Do we file this with our personal taxes or separately?"
A: "A partnership is a separate legal and taxable entity. It files its own return, a 1065."
